Robotic Modelling & Design Skin

Majestic Manta Ray

Inspired by the effortless elegance of the manta ray, this project aims to recreate its fluid form and natural movement using advanced 3D modelling techniques and robotics. The manta ray’s unique shape and swimming patterns offer valuable insights into streamlined design and efficient locomotion. Known for their wide wingspans and graceful underwater gliding, manta rays are filter feeders that navigate with silent, fluid propulsion — characteristics that make them ideal candidates for biomimetic inspiration in robotic design. Their anatomy is not only visually stunning but functionally advanced, with adaptations that allow energy-efficient movement, high manoeuvrability, and non-invasive interaction with their environment. Using Fusion 360, we embarked on a journey of biomimicry — a design approach that learns from nature to solve human challenges. By digitally sculpting the manta ray's anatomy, we aimed to understand the balance between form, structure, and functionality in nature. The ultimate goal was to develop a robotic skin prototype that reflects the manta ray’s graceful movement while exploring potential real-world applications in underwater robotics, design innovation, and sustainable technologies.

Stage 1: Initial Concept and Sketching

Creating an initial sketch is one of the most important early steps in developing any artefact. It helps translate raw ideas into a visual form, making it easier to think through the concept and communicate it clearly to others. In our project, sketching allowed us to explore different layouts, test interaction flows, and catch potential usability issues before any coding began. According to Buxton (2007), sketches are not about beauty or perfection, they are tools for thinking, discovering, and iterating quickly. They also serve as a shared language between creators and stakeholders, enabling better collaboration and feedback (Greenberg et al., 2012). Our initial sketches played a vital role in shaping the final artefact by laying down a clear foundation that guided design and development decisions throughout the project.

Stage 2: Creating the Virtual Prototype

To bring our manta ray concept into a digital form, we used Onshape a cloud-based CAD software known for its real-time collaboration and precision tools. Starting with the basic skeletal structure, we applied surfacing techniques to capture the curvature and fluid form of the manta ray’s wings. The ability to adjust features parametrically made it easier to refine proportions and simulate realistic fin movement. To ensure accuracy, we imported scanned reference models of hand-drawn sketches, allowing us to align the digital design with natural anatomical features. This integration of manual and digital processes not only enhanced the design's authenticity but also reduced trial-and-error during physical prototyping (Singh et al., 2021). By combining CAD modelling with 3D scanning input, we achieved a prototype that was both visually accurate and structurally ready for testing.

Stage 3: Physical Prototype and Testing

Following the completion of the virtual prototype, we progressed to the fabrication of a physical model using fused deposition modelling (FDM) with PLA filament. This rapid prototyping method enabled us to evaluate the structural integrity and articulation of our design in real-world conditions. Initial test prints revealed several issues, such as excessive bulk and limited flexibility in the fin structure. In response, we refined the skeletal framework by reducing its overall thickness and incorporating cut-outs to improve mobility and decrease weight. Hinged joints were also added to replicate the undulating movement characteristic of manta rays. To provide additional rigidity without compromising the lightweight design, certain elements were laser-cut from acrylic, ensuring clean, precise fits for key load-bearing components. Although resin casting was initially considered for enhanced finish and flexibility, it was ultimately excluded due to time constraints and the extended curing period required (Zhang, Kim and Torres, 2019; Walker, 2015). The testing phase was instrumental in validating the model’s balance, aesthetics, and mechanical performance, setting the foundation for further refinement and assembly (Chen, Wang and Zhao, 2020; Lipson and Kurman, 2016).

Reflection/Literature Review

Looking back at the development process, this project has been an eye-opening journey that blended creativity, engineering, and inspiration from nature. We started with hand-drawn sketches to capture the organic form of the manta ray and gradually progressed to a functioning physical prototype using digital tools and rapid prototyping techniques. Each stage gave us an opportunity to learn and refine our approach. Our use of Onshape for digital modelling was particularly beneficial, as it allowed for collaboration and flexible design adjustments. Being able to import reference scans of clay and sketch models helped preserve anatomical accuracy and natural proportions, especially in mimicking the manta ray’s flow and symmetry (Singh, Sharma and Ahuja, 2021). Although we initially intended to use resin casting to enhance surface realism, we had to scale back due to time limitations. Resin moulding, while effective for high-quality finishes, requires longer curing times and careful planning, which we could not accommodate within our schedule (Walker, 2015). The physical prototype brought the design to life. Using laser-cut acrylic for the internal frame and 3D-printed parts, we constructed a lightweight yet strong structure. The servo-driven motion system successfully simulated the flapping motion of manta ray fins, offering a convincing and engaging outcome (Chen, Wang and Zhao, 2020). This stage showed us how digital designs translate into real-world performance and reminded us of the challenges of balancing visual accuracy with mechanical function. Moreover, reflecting on the bio-inspired elements, we realised how observing natural behaviours can inform sustainable and innovative design. Manta rays are not only visually fascinating but also efficient swimmers, and studying their form gave us insight into improving underwater motion systems (Taylor and Roberts, 2023). This appreciation for biomimicry has shaped our mindset as future designers and engineers. In conclusion, the project not only allowed us to develop practical skills in design and fabrication, but it also deepened our understanding of how technology and nature can work together. We are confident that this experience will influence how we approach problem-solving and prototyping in future projects.
