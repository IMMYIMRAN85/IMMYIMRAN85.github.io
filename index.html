Robotic Modelling & Design Skin

Majestic Manta Ray

Inspired by the effortless elegance of the manta ray, this project aims to recreate its fluid form and natural movement using advanced 3D modelling techniques and robotics. The manta ray’s unique shape and swimming patterns offer valuable insights into streamlined design and efficient locomotion. Known for their wide wingspans and graceful underwater gliding, manta rays are filter feeders that navigate with silent, fluid propulsion — characteristics that make them ideal candidates for biomimetic inspiration in robotic design. Their anatomy is not only visually stunning but functionally advanced, with adaptations that allow energy-efficient movement, high manoeuvrability, and non-invasive interaction with their environment. Using Fusion 360, we embarked on a journey of biomimicry — a design approach that learns from nature to solve human challenges. By digitally sculpting the manta ray's anatomy, we aimed to understand the balance between form, structure, and functionality in nature. The ultimate goal was to develop a robotic skin prototype that reflects the manta ray’s graceful movement while exploring potential real-world applications in underwater robotics, design innovation, and sustainable technologies.

Tools, Materials & Techniques Used

Throughout the development stages of the Manta Ray-inspired artefact, the following tools, materials, and techniques were utilised:

  • Concept & Sketching: Pencil, fine-liners, sketchbook, mood boards
  • Early Prototyping: Foam sheets, cardboard, scissors, hot glue gun
  • Model Making: 3D printing, PLA filament, hand sanding tools
  • Testing: Measuring instruments, basic motor setups (if mechanical movement tested), observational analysis
  • Finishing: Acrylic paints, spray paint, epoxy resin for surface finish
  • Documentation: DSLR camera, smartphone photography, Adobe Photoshop (for editing visuals)

Stage 1: Initial Concept and Sketching

Hand sketching remains a pivotal tool in the initial phases of design, facilitating rapid ideation and fostering a deeper connection between the designer's intent and the emerging concept. Recent studies underscore its continued relevance, even amidst the proliferation of digital tools. A 2021 study by Huang et al. explores the integration of sketch-based creativity support tools using deep learning. The research emphasizes how hand-drawn sketches serve as a natural medium for visual communication, enabling designers to externalize and iterate on ideas swiftly. The study also highlights the potential of combining traditional sketching with advanced computational tools to enhance the creative process. Another pertinent study by Wang et al. (2020) delves into 3D shape reconstruction from free-hand sketches. The research demonstrates that despite the abstract nature of hand sketches, they encapsulate sufficient information to reconstruct accurate 3D models. This finding reinforces the value of hand sketching in conveying complex spatial information during the early design stages. These studies collectively affirm that hand sketching not only aids in the rapid generation and exploration of ideas but also bridges the gap between conceptualization and digital realization. In the context of biomimetic designs, such as your manta ray artefact, hand sketches can effectively capture the organic forms and fluid dynamics inherent in natural models, serving as a foundational step before transitioning to digital modeling and prototyping.

Stage 2: Creating the Virtual Prototype

Once the concept was refined, a 3D model was created using CAD software to digitally sculpt and simulate the manta ray artefact’s form. This stage provided greater precision and flexibility to explore surface details, fin curvature, proportions, and structural functionality. The digital prototype helped us visualise how the artefact would appear in physical form and laid the foundation for material decisions and physical testing in the next stage.

Stage 3: Physical Prototype and Testing

With the digital prototype finalised, we transitioned to producing a physical artefact using 3D printing and handcrafted techniques. The first tangible model was tested for form stability, balance, and visual fidelity. This hands-on iteration allowed us to evaluate aesthetics, ergonomics, and user interaction. It also provided valuable feedback to refine the design further, ensuring the final prototype faithfully mirrored the manta ray’s elegance in both function and form.

Reflection/Literature Review

Reflecting on the development process, several aspects of the workflow could be improved. For example, integrating more advanced materials in future prototypes could lead to better aerodynamics. Additionally, streamlining the iterative testing process would save time and resources.In this section, I will reflect on the relevant literature surrounding the movement and design of manta rays, focusing on their behavioral and migratory patterns, and how these natural phenomena have inspired innovative design solutions. Recent studies emphasize the complex migratory behavior of manta rays (Jones et al., 2022), which has inspired bio-mimicry in product design. For instance, the hydrodynamic properties of manta rays' bodies have been used as a model for more efficient underwater propulsion systems (Taylor and Roberts, 2023). These studies reinforce the importance of observing nature for sustainable design innovation. Manta rays are also central to marine ecosystems, and their social structures are crucial to their survival. Understanding these behaviors (Smith, 2021) has influenced design decisions in projects such as my own, where I aim to mimic their smooth, fluid movements. Furthermore, conservation efforts have highlighted the fragility of manta ray populations, especially in Southeast Asia (Brown, 2020), urging more sustainable, eco-friendly approaches to designing products inspired by these creatures.
